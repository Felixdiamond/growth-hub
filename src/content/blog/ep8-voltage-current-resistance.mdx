<BlogSection>
  ## Voltage (V)

  <BlogCallout type="important">
    **What is Voltage?**
    
    Voltage is the electrical pressure or force that pushes electric charges through a circuit.
  </BlogCallout>

  <BlogList>
    <BlogListItem>**Water Analogy** - Like water pressure in a hose</BlogListItem>
    <BlogListItem>**Measurement** - Measured in Volts (V)</BlogListItem>
    <BlogListItem>**Power Source** - Provided by batteries or power supplies</BlogListItem>
    <BlogListItem>**Arduino** - Most Arduino boards use 5V or 3.3V</BlogListItem>
  </BlogList>

  <BlogCallout type="note">
    Just as higher water pressure pushes water more forcefully, higher voltage pushes electric charges with more force.
  </BlogCallout>
</BlogSection>

<BlogSection accent>
  ## Current (I)

  <BlogCallout type="important">
    **What is Current?**
    
    Current is the flow of electric charges through a circuit, similar to water flowing through a pipe.
  </BlogCallout>

  <BlogList>
    <BlogListItem>**Water Analogy** - Like the rate of water flow</BlogListItem>
    <BlogListItem>**Measurement** - Measured in Amperes (A) or milliamps (mA)</BlogListItem>
    <BlogListItem>**Flow Direction** - Moves from higher to lower voltage</BlogListItem>
    <BlogListItem>**Arduino** - Most Arduino pins can handle up to 20mA</BlogListItem>
  </BlogList>

  <BlogCallout type="warning">
    **Safety Note:**
    
    Too much current can damage components or create safety hazards. Always use appropriate resistors!
  </BlogCallout>
</BlogSection>

<BlogSection>
  ## Resistance (R)

  <BlogCallout type="important">
    **What is Resistance?**
    
    Resistance is the opposition to current flow in a circuit, like a narrow section in a water pipe.
  </BlogCallout>

  <BlogList>
    <BlogListItem>**Water Analogy** - Like a bottleneck in the pipe</BlogListItem>
    <BlogListItem>**Measurement** - Measured in Ohms (Ω)</BlogListItem>
    <BlogListItem>**Purpose** - Controls current flow and protects components</BlogListItem>
    <BlogListItem>**Components** - Resistors are the most common way to add resistance</BlogListItem>
  </BlogList>

  <BlogCallout type="note">
    Common resistor values in Arduino projects range from 220Ω to 10kΩ.
  </BlogCallout>
</BlogSection>

<BlogSection accent>
  ## Ohm's Law

  The relationship between voltage, current, and resistance is defined by Ohm's Law:

  <BlogCallout type="important">
    **V = I × R**
    
    <BlogList>
      <BlogListItem>**V** - Voltage in Volts (V)</BlogListItem>
      <BlogListItem>**I** - Current in Amperes (A)</BlogListItem>
      <BlogListItem>**R** - Resistance in Ohms (Ω)</BlogListItem>
    </BlogList>
  </BlogCallout>

  <BlogCallout type="tip">
    **Quick Calculations:**
    
    <BlogList>
      <BlogListItem>**Find Voltage:** V = I × R</BlogListItem>
      <BlogListItem>**Find Current:** I = V ÷ R</BlogListItem>
      <BlogListItem>**Find Resistance:** R = V ÷ I</BlogListItem>
    </BlogList>
  </BlogCallout>
</BlogSection>

<BlogSection>
  ## Practical Example

  Let's calculate the current through an LED circuit:

  <OrderedList>
    <OrderedListItem>
      **Given Values**
      
      <BlogList>
        <BlogListItem>**Arduino Voltage** - 5V from the board</BlogListItem>
        <BlogListItem>**LED Resistor** - 220Ω protection resistor</BlogListItem>
      </BlogList>
    </OrderedListItem>
    <OrderedListItem>
      **Calculate Current**
      
      I = V ÷ R = 5V ÷ 220Ω ≈ 0.023A or 23mA
    </OrderedListItem>
    <OrderedListItem>
      **Check Safety**
      
      23mA is slightly above the recommended 20mA, so we might want to use a 330Ω resistor instead
    </OrderedListItem>
  </OrderedList>

  <BlogCallout type="warning">
    **Component Protection:**
    
    Always include appropriate resistors to protect LEDs and other components from excessive current.
  </BlogCallout>
</BlogSection>
